About Me

I am an Assistant Professor of English Language Teaching and Learning at Brigham Young University–Hawaii within the Faculty of Education and Social Work. My work sits at the intersection of language education, motivation research, and the responsible integration of artificial intelligence in learning environments.

My doctoral research at the University of Liverpool examined the motivational dynamics of EAP learners in transnational English-medium universities — probing how identity, environment, and aspiration shape the language learning journey. Before joining BYU–Hawaii, I served as a Senior Lecturer and Virtual Reality Language Learning Lab Manager at Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool University in Suzhou, China, and as an English Language Fellow with the U.S. Department of State in Shanghai.

I also hold a B.A. in Chinese (minor in Sociology) and a TESOL certificate from Brigham Young University, Provo, as well as a Postgraduate Certificate in Teaching and Supporting Learning in Higher Education (with merit) from Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool University.

Research Interests

My scholarship spans six areas: generative artificial intelligence in SLA, language pedagogy, computer-assisted language learning (CALL), the psychology of language learning, complex dynamic systems, and virtual reality learning environments. I publish in journals including TESOL Quarterly, Computers and Education, Language Learning & Technology, and RELC Journal.

Teaching Philosophy

Inspired by watching my Chinese father-in-law cook — a few simple principles that guide everything.

"I learned from my Chinese father-in-law that cooking can be more than a chore — it can be an art, an opportunity for creative expression and enjoyment. Similarly, teaching need not become monotonous. Teaching is an opportunity for creativity and enjoyment for both teacher and student."

1
Understand Your Students
Just as good cooking begins with understanding your ingredients, good teaching begins with understanding your students — their personality, culture, native language, goals, and how they interact with one another.
2
Visualize the End Result
Good teaching is driven by meaningful, practical goals. It begins by identifying what students should be able to do or know by the end — then reverse-engineering the path to get there.
3
Understand the Underlying Processes
Map the steps: How will you assess success? What curriculum enables it? How do daily lessons and scaffolding support learners on the way to the outcome?
4
Do Not Be Afraid to Experiment
The most successful teachers adapt. Each class is different — there is no one right way to teach. Understanding who your learners are and where they need to go is the compass; method is flexible.
