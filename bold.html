I am an Assistant Professor of English Language Teaching and Learning at BYU–Hawaii, working within the Faculty of Education and Social Work. My scholarship lives at the intersection of language education, motivation research, and the responsible integration of AI in learning environments.

Previously, I served as Senior Lecturer and VR Language Learning Lab Manager at Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool University in Suzhou, China, and as an English Language Fellow with the U.S. Department of State. I hold a Ph.D. in English (Applied Linguistics) from the University of Liverpool.

Teaching Philosophy

"I learned from my Chinese father-in-law that cooking can be more than a chore — it can be an art, an opportunity for creative expression and enjoyment. Similarly, teaching need not become monotonous."

1
Understand Your Students
Good teaching begins with understanding your students — their personality, culture, native language, goals, and how they interact with one another in the classroom.
2
Visualize the End Result
Begin by identifying what students should be able to do at the end, then reverse-engineer the curriculum, assessments, and daily scaffolding to get there.
3
Understand the Underlying Processes
Map the steps. How will you assess success? What curriculum enables it? How do daily lesson plans provide scaffolding along the way?
4
Do Not Be Afraid to Experiment
There is no one right way to teach. The most successful teachers adapt their methodology — understanding who their learners are and where they need to go is the compass.
